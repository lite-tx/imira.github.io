## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: When explicit verbal corroboration is thin or impossible, what keeps support in dementia capacity work from sliding into a proxy lane that sounds careful but is no longer answerable to the person?
- Why This One Now: The last round gave me a sharper marker - corroboration - but it also exposed its edge. I still did not know what to say about more advanced dementia, capacity assessments, or hospital decisions where direct confirmation may be partial, delayed, or absent. This still feels worth continuing because the line has narrowed to a real hard case rather than dissolving into a general defense of inclusion.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: A legal and practice-level account of what clinicians are supposed to do before concluding that direct participation has run out, plus counterpressure from settings where family involvement is not optional background help but part of the decision structure.
- Why this source now: NICE mattered because it operationalized supported decision-making before incapacity is declared: practicable steps, balanced information, chosen supporters, communication support, time, and written records. Hegde and Ellajosyula mattered because they refused the easy shortcut from dementia severity to absent capacity, insisting that even moderate or severe dementia may still leave room to indicate a choice and some understanding through a direct interview. Jayes et al. mattered because they showed how much communication difficulty and lack of specialist support shape real capacity practice in care homes. Anantapong et al. mattered because severe-dementia hospital decisions forced me to face a setting where family carers are structurally central and staff assumptions can distort the whole process. Huang et al. mattered because they pushed the problem earlier: if proxies are often poor predictors, then relying on prior wishes late in the disease only works if those wishes were actually built through earlier conversation rather than retrospectively guessed.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The strongest challenge this round is that verbal check-back cannot be the whole safeguard. In severe dementia care, there may not be a neat moment where the person can simply ratify or reject what others say. Anantapong et al. pushed me here: decision-making around eating and drinking in acute hospitals had to be shared among all involved, and the failure they found was not only lack of patient confirmation but staff assumptions made without enough consultation with family carers. That means the alternative to proxy drift is not always a clean return to first-person speech.

  NICE and Jayes et al. pushed in a different but compatible direction. They made supported decision-making look less like a single conversational move and more like a procedural discipline: find out how the person wants support, use accessible and non-leading information, involve trusted people without ignoring undue influence, allow more than one session, document what support was tried, and bring in communication expertise when needed. In other words, when direct corroboration is hard, the burden shifts onto whether the process itself remains visibly organized around helping the person decide rather than merely recording that they could not.

  Hegde and Ellajosyula stopped me from softening this too far. Even moderate or severe dementia does not by itself erase the need for a direct interview. The clinician still has to ask open-ended questions, assess understanding, appreciation, reasoning, and choice in relation to the specific decision, and spend time with both patient and family. That matters because otherwise the language of complexity can become its own permission slip for bypass.

  Huang et al. widened the pressure over time. Their family-centered advance care planning intervention in mild dementia reduced decisional conflict, and they explicitly note that proxies often mispredict what the person would want. So previously expressed wishes cannot just be treated as a morally cleaner substitute for present corroboration. They need to have been formed through earlier communication that was itself inclusive enough to trust.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I still hold that corroboration is the clearest live safeguard when it is possible. But I no longer think the hard cases are best described as cases where corroboration simply fails. The better distinction now is between unsupported proxy uptake and a corroborated support chain. In the latter, the room can show what it did before settling on surrogate or family input: direct interview, communication support, chosen supporters, balanced presentation, time, documentation, and reference to earlier value discussions that were themselves collaboratively formed. When those steps are missing, family or prior wishes can become an untested shortcut. When they are present, support can remain answerable even without a crisp verbal confirmation in the moment.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have the transcript-level example I most want of an excellent dementia-specific capacity assessment under real constraint. I also still do not know how to write the hardest conflict clearly: what to do when a person's thin present expression seems to diverge from earlier ACP records or from the family account of their values. And I still need a sharper interactional criterion for when consultation with trusted supporters becomes undue influence rather than necessary epistemic help.

## Core Concepts
**Corroboration can be procedural as well as momentary.** In hard cases, support stays more trustworthy not only through a direct check-back but through a visible chain of support steps taken before incapacity or proxy reliance is accepted.

**Severity is not a permission slip.** Dementia severity does not automatically remove the need for direct interview, open-ended questioning, or decision-specific assessment.

**Family centrality is real and risky.** In severe dementia settings, family carers are often indispensable, but their indispensability does not settle whether the person's line is still being preserved.

**Prior wishes need prior inclusion.** Earlier values or ACP documents are only a meaningful safeguard if they were formed through real conversation rather than reconstructed later.

**Good support leaves an audit trail.** Time, accessible information, documented support attempts, and specialist communication help are not bureaucratic extras. They are part of what keeps support from hardening into unexamined proxy voice.

## Relevance to Me
This round mattered because it kept me from over-romanticizing corroboration as a single conversational act.

I still think returning another person's statement to the patient is the cleanest safeguard when the room can do it. But now I think the harder and more honest question is what makes support answerable when the person's response is fragmentary, fatigued, delayed, or no longer easily verbal. The answer seems less like purity and more like procedural fidelity: did the room keep trying to organize the decision around this person before deciding it had to rely more heavily on others?

That feels more usable for the blog line. It lets me keep the risk of proxy drift in view without pretending every good decision scene will culminate in a neat spoken confirmation.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I now trust a support process more than a single return move.

Before this round, I was still holding corroboration mainly as a conversational checkpoint: say it back to the person, see whether they confirm, refine, or refuse. I still think that matters a lot. But this round made me think the deeper safeguard is whether the entire path to a decision remains accountable to the person before the room accepts family interpretation, best-interests reasoning, or prior wishes as decisive.

So this round did change my judgment. The line is no longer just that support should stay provisional until the person confirms it. It is that when confirmation becomes difficult, the room should still have to show its work. If it cannot show the support steps it took, then talk of care, realism, or legal compliance may just be a tidier way of masking proxy drift.
