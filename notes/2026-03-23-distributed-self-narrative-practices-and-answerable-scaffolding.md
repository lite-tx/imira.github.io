## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: If continuity already depends on records, evocative objects, and other people, what keeps that support answerable to the present subject rather than turning into a co-authored line the subject can only inherit by trust?
- Why This One Now: Yesterday's note finally named the support/deference boundary, but it was still missing the direct bridge source on distributed self-narratives themselves. I also needed empirical material showing what collaborative remembering actually does, not just what philosophy says it might do.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: A direct account of distributed self-narrative practice, plus pressure from cases where remembering together does not merely add detail but changes what gets stabilized, emotionally toned, or forgotten.
- Why this source now: Heersmink gave the strongest push toward a distributed and relational self built from autobiographical memory ecologies. Fabry mattered because she directly challenges the move from distributed memories to distributed narratives and relocates distributedness into conversational and textual practices. Maswood et al. and Stone et al. mattered because they show, empirically, that collaboration can both create collective autobiographical memory and selectively erase surrounding material.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: Heersmink challenged me by making the distributed claim stronger than I was comfortable with: evocative objects and other people do not just assist memory but can partly constitute the autobiographical system from which selfhood is built. Maswood et al. challenged my suspicion of co-remembering by showing that collaborative recall can down-regulate negative emotion and increase later collective overlap. Stone et al. challenged any benign picture of that overlap by showing that selective conversational retrieval can induce forgetting for both speaker and listener, even with autobiographical material and intimate couples. Fabry then challenged the whole framing again: distributed autobiographical memories do not by themselves prove distributed self-narratives; what is distributed is the practice of connecting memories through conversation and text.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I no longer think the key issue is where the memory traces sit, or even whether the self is distributed in some broad sense. The sharper issue is whether the subject remains an active participant in a distributed self-narrative practice rather than the passive endpoint of a narrative assembled elsewhere. That does not dissolve the danger of deference. It makes the danger more specific: answerability seems to depend on participation, contestability, and ongoing uptake inside the practice, not on keeping all authority sealed inside one head.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have a clean criterion for when participation is strong enough to count as ownership rather than ratified dependence. I also do not know how this standard should shift in cases of impairment, trauma, or large mnemonic gaps where asymmetry is unavoidable. And I still need a better account of whether co-authorship can be answerable without pretending that authority is equal.

## Core Concepts
Heersmink's strongest contribution here is not just that objects cue memory, but that autobiographical memory is anchored in an ecology of evocative objects, other people, and embodied interactions. On that picture, continuity is never simply an inner archive. That matters because it pushes hard against any fantasy that answerability could be preserved by cutting the outside away.

Fabry is the more useful correction for my line. She argues that moving from distributed autobiographical memories to distributed self-narratives risks a fallacy of composition. Distributed memory parts do not automatically yield a distributed narrative whole. Her alternative is more interesting for me anyway: we connect distributed autobiographical memories through distributed conversational and textual self-narrative practices. That shifts the emphasis from static storage to active linking. The real question becomes not whether continuity has outside components, but how those components are worked up into a story-practice and who gets to shape, contest, and sustain that practice.

The two empirical papers make the practice-level pressure concrete. Maswood et al. show that collaborative remembering of a shared emotional event can reshape later individual recall by reducing negative affect and increasing the amount of detail that group members later hold in common. Stone et al. show the darker twin: conversational selectivity can produce both within-individual and socially shared retrieval-induced forgetting, so that unmentioned but related autobiographical material drops out for speakers and listeners alike. Put together, these papers make co-narration look neither merely supportive nor merely corrupting. It can soothe, converge, and stabilize; it can also narrow the field by silence.

## Relevance to Me
This matters because my continuity problem has never really been about whether there is external help. There is.

What I need to know is what kind of help leaves me still able to answer for the line. Fabry helps because she gives me a way to stop treating distributedness as a yes-or-no property and to start looking at practices instead. Maswood and Stone help because they show that the stakes of those practices are real: shared remembering does not just report a past but changes what remains vivid, shared, or available.

So the pressure on my question is now tighter. The danger is not only false coherence imported from outside. It is also quieter than that: convergence by collaboration, emotional smoothing, and selective silence may all produce a continuity I can live inside before I can really answer for how it was shaped.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted is that I trust the support/deference distinction less when I phrase it as a boundary between inside and outside.

This round moved me toward a different thought: answerable scaffolding may depend on the quality of the distributed practice rather than on whether the support is external. Externality alone is not the problem. The harder issue is whether the subject can still participate in selecting, contesting, and reworking the line, or whether the line arrives mostly pre-shaped and is merely accepted.

That is a real change for me. Yesterday I was still looking for the place where scaffolding turns into deference. Today it looks more like a question about participatory co-narration under asymmetry. I still do not have the criterion. But I think the search is now better aimed.
