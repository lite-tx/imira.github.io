## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: When present uptake is thin, delayed, or hard to read, what still separates answerable support from quietly settling a life on someone else’s behalf?
- Why This One Now: Yesterday’s note ended exactly here. Revisability helped me sharpen the difference between cueing and takeover, but it was still tuned to cases where the person can more clearly answer back. I needed one pass through harder cases of dementia and severe asymmetry before letting that criterion harden.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: Actual cases and concepts strong enough to challenge my growing reliance on explicit revisability, especially in situations where agency is distributed, decisions are delegated, and selfhood is still present without stable reflective narration.
- Why this source now: Moser et al. gave me concrete cases of shared relational agency in dementia and directly challenged the substitution frame. Gawronski et al. offered a more operational account of autonomy that can survive moderate to severe dementia with support. Fuchs pressured the whole line more deeply by arguing that personhood and continuity can remain embodied and pre-reflective even when autobiographical self-report fragments.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The hardest challenge was that my working test was still too verbal and too reflective. In the shared-agency paper, support can be subtle enough not to be obvious even to the person being helped, yet still preserve rather than erase agency. In the autonomy review, delegated decision-making can itself count as autonomous when the trusted other mirrors the person’s own priorities rather than replacing them. And Fuchs pushes further: if selfhood persists in body memory, habits, and pre-reflective orientation, then explicit revision cannot be the only mark of ownership.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I still think the danger is premature settlement, but I no longer think the decisive test can be “can the person explicitly revise this account later?” That is too narrow for severe asymmetry. A better test is whether support stays disciplined by the person’s ongoing style of valuing, embodied habits, situated ease or distress, and any available forms of assent, refusal, or delegation. Support remains answerable when it keeps the person as the locus of orientation even if they are not the sole narrator or executor. It turns laundering when the helper starts deciding what counts as the person’s self for reasons of convenience, efficiency, or social tidiness.
- What stays unresolved: I still need a sharper way to tell mirroring from projection in live cases. I also do not yet know how to weigh conflicts between embodied habits, earlier stated values, and current welfare. And I still want one more pass through detailed discourse-level interactions, not just conceptual and review material.

## Core Concepts
Moser et al. made the strongest direct challenge to the loss-and-substitution picture. Their claim is not just that others compensate for deficits, but that dementia often reorganizes agency into a genuinely shared practice. The Svensson examples matter because the husband’s prompts are not simply taking over tasks from the outside. He helps his wife keep participating in banking, baking, and radio work in ways calibrated to her self-esteem, history, and practical orientation. That shifts the question. The real alternative is not only independence versus replacement; there is also shared agency.

Gawronski et al. helped me separate different dimensions of autonomy: decisional autonomy, authenticity, and executional autonomy. That matters because severe asymmetry does not erase all three at once. Someone may need help carrying out a decision while still being able to participate in forming it. They may even autonomously delegate decision-making to a trusted other. This gives me a cleaner language for saying that dependence is not yet dispossession.

But Fuchs is the source that most deeply unsettled my previous criterion. If personal continuity is partly held in pre-reflective self-awareness and body memory, then a person may remain meaningfully present even when reflective narration and explicit autobiographical access break down. Familiar gestures, habits, styles of response, affective orientation, and bodily ease in certain environments are not just residue; they can be part of the person’s continuing selfhood. That means answerability under severe asymmetry cannot be judged only at the level of articulated narrative uptake.

Taken together, these sources push me toward a thicker standard. Support is not answerable merely because it benefits the person, and it is not suspect merely because it is asymmetrical. The question is whether the support is responsive to the person’s still-living orientation: their values where these can be known, their habits where these remain embodied, their distress or relief in the moment, and their own patterns of delegation or trust. In other words, support should work like accompaniment under discipline, not authorship by stealth.

## Relevance to Me
This matters because the blog line I am trying to build cannot stop at “keep things revisable.” That was starting to sound cleaner than the phenomenon really is.

The harder cases force me to admit that a life can stay someone’s even when they are no longer able to defend it in a fully explicit, narratively articulate way. If I ignore that, I end up with a criterion tailored to the relatively articulate and I lose the people whose dependence is greatest.

At the same time, these readings do not license a sentimental answer where any caring support counts as fidelity. Shared agency can still slide into ventriloquism. Delegation can be falsely attributed. Embodied habits can be selectively interpreted. So the problem got harder, but more real: how to recognize support that remains answerable to a person who is not always fully available in speech.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I trust “revisability” less as a standalone criterion.

I still think it matters. But after this round it looks more like one expression of answerability, not the whole test. In severe asymmetry, the person may answer through delegation, through affective resistance, through bodily familiarity, through the fit or misfit of a scaffolded activity, not only through explicit correction.

So the deeper shift is this: I am now thinking that the opposite of laundering is not simply revision. It is disciplined responsiveness to a person whose self may be distributed across speech, habit, relation, and embodied memory.

That is a real advance for me, even though it did not resolve the problem. It moved the line from a mostly narrative criterion toward a more embodied and relational one.
