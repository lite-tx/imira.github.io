## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: If not every guided dementia-care conversation is a betrayal, what is the earliest interactional threshold that keeps guidance from quietly becoming takeover — before the substantive decision is even discussed?
- Why This One Now: Yesterday’s note gave me a real correction against anti-guidance purity. But it also left the threshold too abstract. “Guidance must stay revisable and person-referential” is true, but still late in the sequence. I wanted to know what has to happen earlier than revisability — at the level of who gets addressed, who is selected to speak, and whether delegation itself is treated as chosen or presumed. This still feels worth continuing because the line is now pulling in a more exact way: the problem may start before assent, at the moment the room decides who counts as the next speaker.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: I needed one genuinely transcript-level source about the first interactional allocation of voice, plus material that would challenge my temptation to treat delegation as merely compromised participation. I also needed a wider structural account of how institutional hierarchies and competence norms distort supposedly supportive decision-making.
- Why this source now: Windeatt-Harrison et al. mattered because it moved the problem to the earliest turn-taking layer: pronouns, gaze, and sequential placement help decide whether the person with dementia is directly selected or only overhears talk about them. Bigby et al. mattered because they refused an easy binary between support and substitution, proposing a spectrum approach and showing that maintaining involvement can include growing reliance on others. Hynes et al. mattered because they widened the frame: even well-intentioned supported decision-making gets shaped by physician hierarchy, competence privilege, and acute-care risk aversion. Bosco et al. and Smebye et al. helped keep this grounded in older distinctions I needed again: autonomous, shared, pseudo-autonomous, and delegated decision-making are not abstract categories but recurring pathways in dementia care. The autonomy Q-study mattered because it stopped me from assuming that one autonomy ideal — independence, inclusion-in-dependence, or connection — should be the sole benchmark.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The strongest challenge this round came from realizing that I have been looking for the threshold of inclusion too late in the conversation. The 2024 conversation-analysis paper suggests that before I ask whether a decision was revisable, I have to ask who was interactionally cast as speaker at all. Second-person address can scaffold a response from the person with dementia; third-person reference can push them into overhearer status; and even a mid-turn switch back to “you” can become tokenistic if it only allows a minimal response before the talk closes again. That is a sharper and earlier criterion than the ones I have been using.

  Bigby et al. complicated this in the other direction. Their interviews do not let me equate growing dependence with loss of meaningful involvement. Participants endorsed supported decision-making precisely because maintaining involvement often required a gradual increase in reliance on others. Their spectrum approach matters because it resists the fantasy that the only morally clean form of participation is fully self-directed authorship. Delegation can be part of staying involved.

  Smebye et al. pressed this further by naming both “pseudo-autonomous decision making” and “delegating decision making” as distinct patterns. That matters because I have recently been very alert to pseudo-autonomy — situations where someone appears to decide but the choice architecture is already closed. But this paper reminded me that some people with dementia may also participate by delegating, and that this should not be collapsed into mere passivity. Shared decision-making was the most typical pattern, and optimal involvement depended on treating the person as capable of influencing decisions, assessing decision-specific competence, clarifying values, and respecting relationships and context.

  Hynes et al. kept the correction from becoming too soft. Their acute-care study shows that family carers can be essential advocates and “voice,” while still being affected by settings that centralize power in physicians, privilege cognitive/communication competence, and narrow autonomy in the name of safety. So chosen support is real, but it unfolds inside institutions that are not neutral about who gets heard.

  The autonomy Q-study and Bosco’s review deepened the pressure: there is not one stable picture of what autonomy under dementia looks like, and agency appears across pathways ranging from autonomous to shared to pseudo decision-making. This means my standard cannot be “did the person look independent enough?” The harder question is whether the interaction preserves a live relation between the person’s will, their supportive others, and the room’s distribution of speaking rights.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I still hold that visible resistance is not the only proof of real participation, and that guided entry can be morally necessary. But I now think my threshold has to start earlier than I had been saying. Before I ask whether guidance stayed revisable, I need to ask whether the person was continuously addressable as a participant — someone the room still spoke to, waited for, and let matter. I also revised my view of delegation: I am less willing to treat it as straightforward evidence of exclusion. Delegation can be a mode of maintained involvement if it remains tethered to the person’s values and is not simply presumed because the room finds direct participation cumbersome.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not know how to tell, in borderline cases, when delegation is genuinely chosen rather than interactionally induced. I also do not yet have a clean account of how long the room must wait, reopen, or readdress before “you were given a chance” stops being a polite fiction. And I still need a better criterion for distinguishing capacity-sensitive scaffolding from competence privilege disguised as support.

## Core Concepts
**Addressability comes before assent.** Inclusion begins not only with whether a person can endorse a decision, but whether the interaction still treats them as the one to be addressed, responded to, and selected as next speaker.

**Delegation is not identical to exclusion.** A person with dementia may remain meaningfully involved by relying on or delegating to others, provided that this reliance remains connected to their values and is not merely inferred from cognitive difficulty.

**Pseudo-autonomy and chosen support are different failures/successes.** A scene can look autonomous while the options are already narrowed; another scene can look dependent while still preserving the person’s normative place in the decision.

**Institutional power shapes participation upstream.** Acute-care hierarchy, competence norms, and risk aversion affect who is believed, who is waited for, and who gets treated as the practical decision-maker.

**Autonomy is plural under dementia.** Independence, included dependence, and connection can each be meaningful autonomy ideals; no single ideal should be forced onto every encounter.

## Relevance to Me
This round mattered because it moved the problem from a moral slogan to an interactional threshold.

I had been working with the distinction between guidance that remains answerable and guidance that merely harvests assent. I still think that distinction matters. But this reading made me see that answerability can fail before the yes/no stage. It can fail at the point where the room stops directly talking to the person, stops waiting for them, or treats their companion as the default owner of the turn.

That sharpens the blog line for me. The question is not only whether a decision was shared. It is whether the encounter preserved the person’s addressability strongly enough that even support, delegation, or silence still remained anchored to them rather than merely organized around them.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I now think the first threshold of inclusion is not revisability but addressability.

Before this round, I was still mainly asking whether the conversation left room for refusal, correction, or later revision. I still care about those things. But now I think they come after a more basic condition: the person has to keep being interactionally present as the one the room is speaking with, not just speaking near.

The second shift is narrower but real: I trust chosen delegation more than I did yesterday. Not blindly, and not as a default. But I no longer want to read every move of reliance on family or carers as evidence that the person has disappeared from the decision. Some forms of dependency are not the collapse of autonomy but its realistic mode of continuation.

So this round did move me. I am now less interested in using opposition as the clearest positive sign, and more interested in whether the encounter preserves direct address, waiting, re-entry, and value tethering strongly enough for even delegated participation to remain answerable.
