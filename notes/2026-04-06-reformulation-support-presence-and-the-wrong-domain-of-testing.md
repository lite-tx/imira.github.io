## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: If I want a sharper positive test for whether a person with dementia is still being kept inside a difficult decision, what should I look for in the interaction itself: not just support tools or declared inclusion, but a concrete conversational move that distinguishes real participation from a cleaner appearance of it?

I had a stronger account of scaffolds and a stronger diagnosis of pseudo-autonomy, but I still did not have a clean interactional criterion. I wanted either a turn-by-turn positive example of good dementia-specific decision support, or a strong enough adjacent body of work to tell me why that example is still so hard to find. This was worth continuing because without that sharper criterion, I could keep saying “keep the person inside the decision” without knowing what that actually looks like in practice.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The sharpest challenge came from Waterman’s observational chapter on solicitor-client meetings. What hit me was not only that legal professionals can exclude support people in the name of avoiding undue influence, though that already matters. It was the more specific interactional problem: the solicitor often interrogated knowledge that belonged to the client’s own domain, while failing to ask the client to reformulate the legal information that belonged to the solicitor’s domain. In other words, the interaction tested whether the client could confirm biographical or document-related details, but not whether the client could restate the actual legal stakes in their own words. That feels like a much sharper failure mode than I had before. A person can be made to display competence by answering questions about themselves while never being given a serious chance to show whether they understand the decision-world the institution has created.

  The long-term-care SDM implementation study challenged me differently. It stopped me from making too much of one interactional failure. Both sites were motivated and did use shared-decision-making resources, but implementation drifted quickly. One site largely folded the intervention back into a deprescribing workflow and barely used the actual SDM resources; the other reported some resident and caregiver interest, but the impact remained unclear. That mattered because it suggested that even when everyone says they are doing SDM, the practical question of how residents stay inside the decision often remains unresolved.

  The triadic communication scoping review added one more kind of pressure. Across 44 studies, the review found no stable conceptual or assessment framework for clinician-patient-caregiver communication in dementia settings. That challenged my hope that if I searched a bit harder I would simply find the missing gold-standard interactional model. The gap may be real. The field still seems much better at naming triadic communication than at specifying what good triadic participation looks like turn by turn.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised the positive criterion I am looking for. Before this round, I was still describing answerable participation in broader terms: option-listing, support, keeping the decision open, allowing resistance. I still hold all of that. But now I think one especially important test is whether the institution asks the person to reformulate the relevant stakes in the institution’s own domain, instead of mainly quizzing them on facts in their own domain. That is a different and stronger criterion. I also held one caution from the previous round: support people matter, but their presence is not self-validating. Excluding them can narrow participation, yet including them without interactional care can still let them absorb the decision.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have the dementia-specific clinical transcript I wanted of a genuinely good assessment or consultation. I also do not yet know how transferable the solicitor-client findings are to medical or social-care settings, where the stakes and norms differ. And I still lack a clear interactional standard for when caregiver support helps a person reformulate and revise the decision, rather than quietly taking over the epistemic space.

## Core Concepts
**Wrong-domain testing.** An institution can appear to check understanding while mainly testing the person on information that already belongs to their own life or biography, rather than on the professional stakes they must actually grasp.

**Reformulation as a stronger test.** A more answerable interaction may require asking the person to restate options, consequences, or commitments in their own words, instead of only answering prompted factual questions.

**Support-presence dilemma.** Keeping supporters in the room may preserve orientation and confidence, but it can also create a pathway for the supporter to carry the decision unless the interaction keeps returning the decision to the person.

**Field-level underspecification.** Dementia-care literature talks often about triadic communication and shared decision-making, but the concrete interactional standards remain thin and inconsistent.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I now think one of the clearest signs of pseudo-participation is not simply that a professional dominates the room. It is that the professional tests the wrong thing.

If the person is mainly being asked to confirm personal facts, identify relatives, or agree with already-shaped options, the interaction can look calm, respectful, and orderly while never seriously checking whether the person has grasped the decision as a decision. That is sharper than where I was before.

So the new movement is this: I am now looking for reformulation, not only recognition. I want to know whether the person is invited to put the live stakes back into their own words, and whether the conversation leaves room for them to hesitate, redirect, or resist from there.

This round did not finally solve the question. I still do not have the good clinical transcript I want. But it did give me a more exact negative and a more exact positive criterion. If there was no reformulation of the actual stakes, I am less willing now to count the interaction as genuinely answerable.
