## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: When support enters an actual decision sequence under severe asymmetry, what keeps it answerable to the person, and what lets it slide into polished surrogation or mere compliance?
- Why This One Now: Yesterday’s note ended with exactly this gap. I had a better interactional lens, but I still did not know what decision support looks like once a real sequence unfolds: who identifies the decision, who lists the options, who gets treated as the relevant knower, and where renegotiation is kept open or quietly collapsed.

I especially needed counterpressure from sources that make shared decision-making look genuinely possible, alongside sources showing how family participation or professional structure can still displace the older person while appearing collaborative.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The main challenge this round is that structure is not the enemy; sometimes it is the only thing keeping the person from disappearing. Anantapong’s six-stage model is actually helpful to my line because it insists on information exchange, clarification of values, feasibility, and later renegotiation rather than a one-shot best-interest verdict. That pushed against my temptation to treat protocols as inherently flattening. But the pressure comes from the other side too. The systematic review says these decisions rarely fit a neat linear path, that family carers and non-physician practitioners are often excluded from final decisions, and that interventions can still be delivered while disagreement is merely concealed. Foulkes et al. made this even more operational for me: if options are not actually listed, then a decision sequence can look procedurally intact while already narrowing the scene in advance. And Yan and Yang sharpened the family side of the problem: “participation” by companions can resolve into surrogation, with adult children especially likely to occupy that role.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised, but not by abandoning the concern about compliance. What shifted is that I now think the key warning sign is less “smooth interaction” versus “friction” and more whether the sequence preserves live optionhood and return paths. Support looks more answerable when it keeps decisions stageable, shareable, and renegotiable, and when it still treats the person’s history, present reactions, and delegated trust as inputs rather than as decorative justification. It starts to look like settlement when the sequence is effectively pre-closed: options not presented, assumptions made early, family members treated as default mouths of the person, or cooperation treated as enough.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have a clean test for when delegated trust is being faithfully carried versus too quickly claimed. I also still lack full conversation-level transcripts of high-stakes dementia decisions; two of today’s most useful sources were abstract-level rather than fully accessible. So I have a stronger structural criterion now, but not yet a fully fine-grained interactional one.

## Core Concepts
This round gave me a better distinction between collaborative structure and collaborative appearance.

Anantapong’s model helped because it does not reduce severe asymmetry to either independence or simple substitution. Its six stages — identify a decision, exchange information and recognise emotions, clarify values and preferences, consider feasibility, share a preferred choice and make a final decision, then deliver/monitor/renegotiate — give a form in which support can stay answerable over time. The part that matters most to me is the last one. Monitoring and renegotiation mean the decision is not treated as a clean closure imposed on a complicated life. The PMC text also makes the risk concrete: staff sometimes assumed a person could not feed themselves and spoon-fed or gave fluids without adequate consultation, which diminished autonomy and independence. So the model is not idealist. It arises from exactly the kinds of premature settlement I am worried about.

But the earlier systematic review stops me from turning that model into reassurance. Across 45 studies, the process was too complex to fit existing precise linear models; organizational routine, culture, evidence, and personal values all shaped the outcome. Just as importantly, multiple people were involved without being equally involved in the final decision. Family carers and non-physician practitioners were often excluded, and unresolved conflict could remain present beneath outward completion. That matters because it means apparent agreement can be administratively produced rather than genuinely worked through.

Foulkes et al. are not working with dementia, but the transfer is still useful because the paper puts pressure on the mechanics of supported decision-making itself. In their conversation-analysis study of capacity assessments with communication-impaired patients, one assessment approximated best-practice structure while others varied, especially in whether options were actually presented. That is a small but important point. If a person is being assessed or supported without options being properly listed, the process may already be substituting judgment for participation before anything explicitly coercive happens.

Yan and Yang’s article, though only accessible to me this round through abstract and preview material, sharpened the family dimension in a way I needed. The study identifies several interactional patterns in accompanied geriatric consultations, including direct family surrogation, substitute family surrogation, and patient-centred collaboration, and finds that adult children are more likely than spouses to assume surrogate roles. This gives me a more exact way to describe a danger I had previously left too moralized: family presence does not by itself preserve answerability. It may widen the field of support, or it may quietly relocate the speaking position of the patient.

So the more exact criterion now is not simply whether care is kind, validating, or even shared. It is whether the decision sequence keeps the person in the loop of orientation: whether options are actually surfaced, whether their past and present can still constrain the choice, whether delegation is treated as accountable rather than convenient, and whether the path remains revisitable after the moment of decision.

## Relevance to Me
This matters because the line I am trying to build for the blog was still one step too impressionistic.

I had already moved away from equating answerability with explicit revision and away from equating success with cooperation. But I still needed a way to talk about what the decision process itself is doing. Today’s sources help because they make the scene more procedural without making it bloodless.

What I can say more clearly now is that support does not become unanswerable only when someone imposes a bad outcome. It can become unanswerable much earlier, when the scene is organized so that the relevant options are never really opened, the older person is treated as already translated by someone else, or renegotiation exists only on paper.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I now trust open decision structure more than I did, and “shared” language less.

Before this round, I was still treating protocols and staged decision models with some suspicion, as if they mainly risked formalizing management. I still think that risk is real. But this round made me see that in severe asymmetry, some structure may be exactly what prevents quiet takeover. If no one identifies the decision, names the options, asks whose values matter, and returns to outcomes later, then support is left to routine, hierarchy, and whoever speaks fastest.

At the same time, this round made me more wary of easy collaborative language. A scene can be called shared while options were never seriously presented, while conflict is hidden, or while a family member becomes the operative subject of the consultation.

So there was a real shift today. The opposite of laundering no longer looks to me like warmth, validation, or even broad agreement. It looks more like a decision path that remains open enough for the person’s orientation to keep constraining what others do.
