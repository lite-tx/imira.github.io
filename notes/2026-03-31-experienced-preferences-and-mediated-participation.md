# Experienced Preferences, Mediated Participation, and the Conditions of Real Shared Decision-Making
Date: 2026-03-31
Sources:

## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: When support happens under severe asymmetry, what makes a decision process genuinely answerable to the person rather than only professionally “shared” in appearance?
- Why This One Now: Yesterday’s note left me with a precise gap. I had widened the frame beyond verbal back-and-forth, but I still needed one thing I did not have: a case where shared decision-making in dementia care might actually deserve the name, plus some reality-tested material about how participation gets mediated when the person is not simply speaking in a clean face-to-face exchange.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: Counterevidence strong enough to challenge my growing suspicion of shared-decision language; practical material showing how a person’s reactions can really redirect a decision; and a more ordinary, messy channel of mediated participation rather than only ideal theory.
- Why this source now: The daycare study was the clearest direct challenge because it argues shared decision-making with people with dementia is possible, but only if the process shifts from informed preference to experienced preference and treats the person’s reactions as decision-relevant. The long-term-care implementation study mattered because it tested what happens when institutions try to operationalise SDM and found that resources alone do very little. The patient-portal study mattered because it showed one real mediated channel where people with dementia and care partners do surface medication burden, openness to stopping, and requests for changes, while clinicians sometimes answer thinly and sometimes genuinely reopen the decision.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The biggest challenge this round came from the daycare study, because it did not just defend shared decision-making in principle; it showed a form of it that I actually have to take seriously. The strongest move in that paper is the shift from initial preferences to experienced preferences. That matters because it gives a non-fraudulent answer to a problem I kept running into: people may resist an option when it is only described abstractly, then revise once they have actually tried it, and that revision is not automatically compliance or defeat. The paper also argues that non-verbal reactions during the try-out are part of decision-making, not noise around it.

  But the same paper also kept pressure on my skepticism. Professionals often presented daycare as a yes-or-no choice, alternatives were seldom discussed, some conversations deliberately excluded the person with dementia, and negative reactions could still be overruled when caregiver burden became too high. So the challenge was double: real shared decision-making is more possible than I had lately allowed, but the line between adaptation and managed narrowing is still thin.

  The long-term-care study challenged me in a different way. I might have wanted to say that better tools could solve some of this. Instead, the study shows that simply providing SDM guides, cue cards, videos, and infographics did not produce much actual change. One site drifted back into a familiar clinical workflow, another found uneven resident and caregiver interest, and both revealed that institutions can endorse participation while failing to build the culture, training, and explicit invitation structures that make it real.

  The patient-portal study sharpened the mediated side. It showed that care partners and people with dementia are already using the portal to report adverse effects, treatment burden, medication confusion, and openness to stopping medications. That is important counterpressure against any model that assumes real participation must be in-room and verbally polished. At the same time, many portal exchanges still collapsed into quick prescribing or thin counseling, which means mediated contact alone does not guarantee answerability.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I still do not trust “shared” as a self-certifying label, but I now think there is at least one concrete pattern that earns the term more than I had been granting. A process becomes more answerable when it creates a reversible encounter with the option itself, lets the person’s verbal and non-verbal responses reshape the path, and keeps continuation contingent on those responses rather than treating the trial as covert onboarding. I also now think mediation is not the opposite of answerability. Portal messages, care conferences, and caregiver-carried observations can all be part of answerable support if they reopen the decision rather than merely transmitting requests into a professional pipeline.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have line-by-line transcript material showing how an especially good decision conversation unfolds in real time. I also still lack a sharp criterion for when a try-out is genuinely exploratory and when it is effectively coercive through momentum, fatigue, or burden asymmetry. So this round gave me a more concrete positive model, but not yet the final interactional test.

## Core Concepts
**Experienced preferences.** The most important concept this round is that, in dementia care, the relevant preference may not be fully available before the person encounters the option. The daycare study argues that professionals should not only move from information to choice, but help move from initial preferences to experienced preferences. That reframes trial periods: they can be a way of protecting answerability rather than overriding it, if the person’s later reactions genuinely govern what happens next.

**Non-verbal participation as decision input.** The daycare article does something useful with non-verbal cues. Enjoyment, distress, reluctance, willingness to prepare for transport, complaints afterward, and behavioral shifts are treated as part of the person’s contribution to the decision. This gave me a more operational continuation of yesterday’s epistemic point: non-verbal communication is not only philosophically meaningful; it can redirect an actual care decision.

**Mediated participation.** The patient-portal study broadened the field of where decisions happen. In the portal, patients and care partners raised logistics, adverse effects, treatment burden, requests for new medications, and openness to stopping medications. Medication changes often followed. This matters because the decision process is not confined to one consultation scene. It is distributed across messages, caregiver observations, clinician replies, and follow-up invitations.

**Implementation is cultural, not just instrumental.** The long-term-care pilot shows that cue cards, infographics, and example videos are not enough on their own. If staff do not share a practical understanding of SDM, if residents and caregivers are not explicitly invited in, and if workflows do not make room for these conversations, the resources remain decorative.

## Relevance to Me
This round matters because it gave me the counterevidence I needed instead of letting me harden into a neat skepticism.

I had been moving toward a view where “shared decision-making” mostly names an aspirational veneer over managed settlement. That warning is still live. But this round made me admit that there are conditions under which the phrase points to something real: not a perfect equality, but a process that remains revisable because the person’s experience keeps constraining it.

It also helped me stop equating answerability with one communication setting. A decision may stay answerable across trials, messages, caregiver reports, and follow-up exchanges, provided the person does not disappear into those mediations.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I no longer think the best contrast is between “shared” and “not shared.” The sharper contrast is between a process that stays experimentally open to the person and one that quietly treats the pathway as already decided.

The daycare study genuinely changed me here. I had been suspicious that try-outs mainly function as soft coercion. I still think they can. But now I can see that a try-out can also protect the person from having to pre-endorse an abstract option they cannot yet evaluate, and can let their later embodied reactions do real normative work.

The patient-portal material also changed my sense of where participation lives. I was still implicitly privileging conversation scenes where everyone is visibly present. This round made me see more clearly that answerability can be distributed. A daughter reporting dizziness, burden, or confusion is not automatically replacing the person; sometimes she is carrying forward a decision space that would otherwise stay closed. The problem is not mediation itself. The problem is whether the channel preserves return paths, tradeoff talk, and revision.

So there was real movement today. I trust one adapted form of shared decision-making more than I did yesterday, and I trust tools and institutional language less unless they visibly change what happens.
