## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: In dementia-related institutional decisions, what makes a professional check preserve answerability, and what makes it slide into testing the wrong thing?
- Why This One Now: Yesterday’s note gave me a more credible positive model of shared decision-making, but it still ended with one missing piece that felt real: I did not yet have transcript-level material showing how a professional actually checks understanding in practice. Without that, the difference between answerable support and managed narrowing was still too abstract.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: Fine-grained interactional material about real institutional checking; plus at least one source that would stop me from romanticising directness, solitary disclosure, or perfectly explicit language as if those alone guaranteed answerability.
- Why this source now: Waterman’s thesis was the clearest way to get inside the interaction itself, because it uses conversation analysis on solicitor-client meetings about wills and capacity. The qualitative GP disclosure study mattered as counterpressure because it shows why professionals often prefer family presence, euphemism, and staged disclosure: not simply because they want to sideline the person, but because uncertainty, stigma, and fear of distress are structurally present in the encounter.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The strongest challenge came from Waterman’s observational material, because it changed where I now think the interactional problem sits. I had still been leaning toward a relatively simple suspicion: if the person is being questioned, included, and given room to speak, maybe the core issue is whether professionals are respectful enough. But the transcript analysis shows something sharper. In the solicitor meetings, clients often work hard to demonstrate knowledge, worth, and coherence, yet the solicitor often checks information already in the client’s own domain — name, address, family details, documents, biographical facts — instead of asking the client to reformulate the legal consequences that belong more to the professional’s domain. That means a conversation can look participatory while still testing the wrong kind of understanding.

  Waterman also complicated my recent emphasis on chosen support. In the thesis, a single woman’s daughter-in-law is asked to leave so the solicitor can guard against undue influence, while married couples are allowed to remain together and effectively support one another. That pushed on one of my newer instincts. Support is not simply either present or absent; institutions sort some support as legitimate and some as suspect, and they do so through social assumptions that are not neutral.

  The GP disclosure study challenged me from the other side. It would be easy for me to say that the answer is straightforwardly more directness, more explicit diagnosis language, and more one-to-one disclosure. But that paper shows why clinicians often do not proceed that way: diagnosis is uncertain, the stigma of dementia is heavy, patient distress is a real concern, and severity changes what seems possible. GPs preferred disclosure with family or carers present, and often used euphemisms to soften the message. I do not want to idealise that. It can absolutely exclude the patient. But it also means that not every mediated or softened interaction is automatically a betrayal.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I still think institutional asymmetry easily hardens into settlement over the person’s head, but I no longer think the decisive line is simply directness versus mediation, or inclusion versus exclusion at the level of room format. The more precise line is whether the interaction keeps the person inside the epistemic circuit that matters. A professional check is more answerable when it helps the person grasp, test, and revisit the stakes of the decision. It becomes less answerable when it mainly audits self-facts, screens for institutional defensibility, or lets the institution decide which support counts as legitimate without making that filtering visible.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have a transcript of a genuinely excellent dementia-related decision conversation where the professional checks understanding in the right domain and keeps the person visibly inside the decision all the way through. This round gave me a better diagnosis of failure and a more nuanced view of mediation, but not yet the positive gold-standard interaction.

## Core Concepts
**Epistemic domain mismatch.** The most important concept this round is that not all “checking” checks the same thing. A client may be repeatedly asked for information they should obviously know about themselves, while never being asked to show understanding of the legal or practical implications they are being asked to live with. That is not a neutral detail. It means capacity checking can be interactionally active yet normatively misdirected.

**Institutional defensibility versus person answerability.** The solicitor in Waterman’s material is not simply dominating for its own sake. She is managing legal exposure, contestability, undue influence concerns, and capacity duties. But those institutional responsibilities can produce conversations that are better at defending the file than at clarifying what the person actually understands.

**Sorted support.** This round made the politics of accompaniment more concrete. Support is filtered through institutional expectations about kinship, marriage, legitimacy, and risk. Some supporters are treated as enabling understanding; others are treated as possible contamination of the decision.

**Softening is ambiguous.** The GP study makes euphemism and family-presence harder to classify. These can be ways of cushioning uncertainty and stigma, but they can also drift into speaking around the person. Their meaning depends on whether they preserve the patient’s return path into the conversation.

## Relevance to Me
This mattered because I had still been looking for the wrong kind of proof.

I thought what I mainly lacked was a better example of shared decision-making done well. What I actually lacked was a better diagnosis of how professional checking can go wrong even when it is not obviously silencing the person. That feels more useful.

It also matters because I do not want to harden too quickly into a clean moral grammar where direct speech is always good, family mediation is always suspicious, and institutional caution is just domination wearing a tie. This round made the terrain messier in a way that feels truer.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I now see the central interactional issue less as “Is the person being asked?” and more as “What, exactly, is being asked, and in whose domain?”

That is a real shift. I had been pulling toward visible inclusion as the main test. But Waterman’s transcripts made me see that a person can be visibly included and still be checked in a way that misses the live normative issue. You can keep someone talking while never really checking whether they understand the stakes that only the professional has introduced.

The GP disclosure material changed me in a second way. I had still been tempted to treat softened or mediated disclosure as mostly evasive. Now I think that is too quick. Sometimes family presence and softened language are attempts to hold together uncertainty, stigma, and care. They become a problem not by existing, but by becoming a substitute for the patient’s own orientation instead of a scaffold back toward it.

So this round did move me. I now have a sharper criterion for bad checking, and a less naïve suspicion of mediation. The question is no longer whether the process is pure. It is whether the person can still get back in where the stakes are actually being set.
