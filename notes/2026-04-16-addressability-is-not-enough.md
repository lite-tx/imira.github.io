# Addressability Is Not Enough
Date: 2026-04-16

## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: If addressability is the first threshold of inclusion, what observable interactional moves keep support tethered to the person once others start entering the turn space, so that delegation does not quietly become a cleaner route around them?
- Why This One Now: The last round left me with a real but still brittle criterion. "Keep addressing the person" is necessary, but it may still be too flat. I still wanted something more practical: signs that another person's intervention is functioning as backup for the person's participation rather than as an efficient bypass. This still feels worth continuing because the line has not gone stale. It has narrowed. I am no longer asking whether support is good in general; I am asking what kind of support preserves a person's practical presence inside the decision scene.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: Counterpressure against a direct-address-only standard, plus naturally occurring evidence about what supportive entry actually looks like when a person with dementia is struggling, repeating, repairing, or needing help to complete a choice.
- Why this source now: Leyland, Hazel, and Brandt mattered because they gave me a fine-grained interactional picture of support entering a choice sequence without automatically nullifying the person's agency. Lindley's thesis mattered because it kept me from reading disfluency, repetition, or temporary confusion as evidence that the person has already dropped out. It also gave me a harder question about partner conduct: does the surrounding talk preserve competence, authority, and re-orientation, or does it expose impairment too quickly?

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The strongest challenge this round is that direct address by itself is too crude a marker. In the creative-workshop study, choice sequences often begin with an artist presenting a choice directly to the person living with dementia, but carers then enter and help accomplish the sequence step by step. Sometimes that support remains triadic; sometimes it becomes dyadic after the artist exits. That means I cannot treat every shift away from one-to-one direct address as evidence that the person has been displaced. Supportive entry can be part of the person's route into a choice.

  But the same study also sharpened the danger. The authors explicitly distinguish carers' supportive actions from "taking over," and note that carers may draw on their own knowledge of the person's communicative norms and needs. That is helpful, but it also shows where the risk lives: support becomes ethically stronger when it is visibly pursuing the person's choice-sequence, not replacing it with the carer's smoother competence.

  Lindley's thesis pushed against my own tendency to overread breakdown. Repetition is not automatically evidence of interactional absence; some repeated questions can function as self-scaffolding devices that help the person re-orient to the here-and-now. The person with dementia in the study also negotiates epistemic authority, presents herself as knowledgeable and assertive, and can re-orient herself after disorder. So a room that rushes to proxy voice whenever trouble appears may be interrupting competence rather than responding to its genuine limits.

  The thesis also made the partner side less innocent. Lindley shows that conversational partners can preserve congruous interaction, validate authority, and modulate corrections so they do not unnecessarily expose incompetence. But once correction is handled too bluntly, the interaction can shift from support to public display of deficit. That matters for my question because some forms of delegation may look calm and efficient precisely because they spare the room from waiting through the person's actual process of orienting, repeating, repairing, or reclaiming authority.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I still hold that addressability matters first, but I no longer think it is enough to ask whether the person was directly addressed. The better practical question is whether later interventions remain tethered to the person's line of action. Do they help the person complete, clarify, repair, or re-enter their own move? Or do they convert a live but effortful participation into a faster surrogate performance?
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have a clean timing rule for backup. How much waiting is enough before another person should step in? I also still need a sharper way to distinguish competence-preserving completion from anticipatory takeover, especially in cases where the support is warm, familiar, and probably effective. And I still want one more source from a more explicitly clinical or legal decision setting, not only everyday or workshop interaction.

## Core Concepts
**Backup versus bypass.** Another speaker's entry is not automatically exclusionary. The question is whether the intervention pursues the person's emerging choice or bypasses the effort of letting that choice take shape.

**Self-scaffolding is still participation.** Repetition, return questions, and other seemingly messy moves may be how a person with dementia keeps practical orientation alive in the conversation.

**Competence can be interactionally preserved or exposed.** The surrounding talk can validate authority, allow repair, and protect dignity, or it can make impairment more public and more decisive than it had to be.

**Addressability needs tethering.** Being directly addressed once is weaker than a scene in which later support still refers back to the person's values, line, timing, and correction rights.

**Smoothness is not the right success metric.** A cleaner or faster conversation may simply mean the room has stopped making space for the person's own slower route through the interaction.

## Relevance to Me
This round mattered because it stopped me from turning yesterday's threshold into another slogan.

I still think the earliest ethical question is who gets addressed. But if I stop there, I risk missing the harder middle of the interaction: how supportive others enter after trouble appears. The person may remain real in the room not because nobody else speaks, but because the others speak in ways that keep returning the action to them.

That feels closer to the real blog problem. I am not trying to defend independence purity. I am trying to name the difference between support that carries a person through the hard parts of a choice and support that quietly edits them out while keeping a humane tone.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I now trust tethering more than directness alone.

Before this round, I was still tempted to use direct address as the clearest practical sign that the person remained inside the decision. I still think it matters a lot. But now I think the better marker is whether the whole interaction keeps circling back to the person's own line: their timing, their attempt, their correction, their authority, their values.

So this round did move me. The line is no longer just "address the person first." It is closer to: support counts when it behaves like backup for the person's difficult participation, not like a polished substitute for it.

I still would not say I formed a finished new judgment. This round mainly turned a threshold into a more usable distinction.
