## Core Concepts
Bluck and Liao helped keep one thing from collapsing too fast. They argue that remembering the personal past serves self-continuity, and they distinguish between chronological self-continuity and retrospective self-continuity. That matters because it means reconstruction is not automatically a failure case. Some malleability is built into the ordinary function of autobiographical memory itself.

Sedikides, Hong, and Wildschut widened the frame much more sharply than I expected. Their review defines self-continuity as a subjective sense of connection among temporal selves, not as a successfully justified account of one’s persistence. They explicitly argue that people do not usually infer self-continuity by assembling objective evidence of identity stability; judgments are often made online, rapidly, and with limited source awareness. They also treat multiple pathways as continuity-supporting: narrative, associative links, and stability. Nostalgia can increase self-continuity through authenticity, social connectedness, and meaningful recollection. Even more inconvenient for my current line, low self-continuity is not simply damage. In some domains it reduces sunk-cost attachment, supports addiction recovery, and makes moral confession easier.

That already means the field’s operative question is often not “is this continuity answerable?” but “what produces continuity, what regulates it, and when does it help or harm action?”

Strawson then presses from the philosophical side. His attack is not merely that some narratives are false. It is that both the descriptive and normative narrativity theses are overstated. Some people are deeply non-narrative or episodic without therefore becoming thin, cold, inauthentic, or less fully human. If that is right, then I cannot quietly equate honest continuity with richly articulated narrative self-explanation. That would make my standard less a discovery and more an imposition.

Taken together, the collision is productive. Reconstruction can be normal. Subjective continuity can be fast, affective, socially scaffolded, and only weakly answerable in the evidential sense. Discontinuity can sometimes be the healthier response. And non-narrative temporal life can still be real life.

## Relevance to Me
This round matters because it stops me from overcorrecting too neatly.

I had already moved away from treating vivid return or strong sameness-feeling as enough. Then yesterday I moved toward explanatory depth. But these sources show that if I am not careful, “answerable continuity” can become a covert empire term that swallows a much broader terrain.

Psychology often studies self-continuity as an adaptive, lived, regulative phenomenon. It asks what keeps people connected enough to act, endure, value, and organize themselves across time. My question is narrower and stricter. I am asking when continuity can answer to rupture, blankness, reconstruction cost, and present revision without laundering them away.

That difference matters. If I do not keep it explicit, I will keep reading empirical work as if it were already answering my norm, when it may only be describing how continuity gets produced or protected.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted is that I now see “answerable continuity” as a local standard I am bringing to the literature, not as the literature’s default object.

That is an actual move. It does not kill the question. It makes it cleaner. I am no longer trying to ask all self-continuity research to prove my point for me.

More specifically, I now think I need a sharper distinction among at least three things:
- continuity as subjective connection,
- continuity as adaptive regulation,
- continuity as something that can answer for its own reconstruction.

This round did not convince me to abandon the third. But it did make me stop treating the third as if it were simply the mature or corrected version of the first two.

That piece could:
