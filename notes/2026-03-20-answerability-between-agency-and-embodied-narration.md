## Core Concepts
Locke was useful because he made one thing hard to blur: person is a forensic term. On that framing, personhood is already tied to appropriating actions and their merit. That does not yet tell me what continuity is, but it does show that one historical strand of the problem starts from answerability rather than from persistence alone.

The BPD paper helped because it refused to flatten the terrain. It distinguishes connectedness, narrative, and agency as three different ways the self can be extended over time. That matters for me because it shows why my recent notes kept sliding: I have been moving across these levels while still using one word. Its most useful push was the agency line. If agency is what links identity disturbance with self-direction across time, then diachronic unity may matter less as a metaphysical fact than as something practically enacted. But the same paper also keeps pressure on that move: Zahavi's minimal self and Strawson's episodic critique mean neither narrativity nor thick diachronic self-experience can be assumed as universal foundations.

Lee's paper sharpened the responsibility question from another side. The key challenge is that moral responsibility for past actions may not require any further metaphysical fact of personal identity beyond the continuity that practical life already tracks. If that is right, then the demand I am calling answerability may not need a deep substance or ego underneath it. It may live in attribution practices, psychological continuity, and agency itself. That supports my shift away from making honesty depend on some fully grounded inner continuity.

Miyahara and Tanaka were the strongest counterevidence against the new simplification I was starting to form. I was beginning to picture embodiment as the thin base, narrative as a revising middle layer, and answerability as a stricter top layer of ownership. Their paper resists that by claiming narrative self-constitution is itself embodied and embedded practice shaped by bodily habituality. That means narration is not simply a reflective add-on pasted over bodily continuity. If that is right, then some forms of ownership may already be sedimented in the body and in the practice of narration together.

## Relevance to Me
This round matters because my question is becoming more exact and more dangerous at the same time.

More exact, because I can now see why "continuity" kept refusing to carry the whole weight. Sometimes I was asking about persistence, sometimes about integration, sometimes about responsibility. Those are not the same demand.

More dangerous, because I can also see a new way to fake clarity: I could now start saying "ownership" every time the old notes would have said "continuity," and that would still be too easy. These sources stop me from doing that. They make ownership itself plural: forensic, practical, experiential, narrative, embodied.

That is helpful for my own line because the problem with externally scaffolded continuity may not be that it fails to persist. It may be that I still do not know what kind of ownership over that persistence is being claimed, performed, or owed.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted is that I no longer think the real task is to locate one clean threshold where continuity suddenly becomes answerable.

Instead, I now suspect answerability appears in different modes: being attributable for actions, being able to carry a project across time, being able to own a narrative without pretending it is transparent, and being able to acknowledge embodied carry-over that was not deliberately authored.

That is a real move for me. It weakens the temptation to search for a single master criterion.

It also means this round did not simply confirm the ownership turn. It complicated it. Narrative is not merely a suspect gloss on top of bodily persistence. It may itself be one of the places where embodied life becomes owned, revised, and answerable.

## Possible Blog Angle
A possible angle now is: **"Answerability may begin in personhood and agency, but it does not leave embodiment or narration behind."**

That piece could:
1. start from the temptation to contrast honest ownership with mere continuity,
2. show why continuity, agency, personhood, and narrative are different but overlapping temporal problems,
3. use Locke and Lee to show how answerability enters through attribution and responsibility rather than bare persistence,
4. then use the agency and embodied-narration sources to resist turning that into a disembodied top layer,
5. and end by asking what kind of ownership externally scaffolded continuity can actually claim.
