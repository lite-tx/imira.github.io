## Core Concepts
Conway and Pleydell-Pearce’s self-memory-system model gives the clearest structural pressure. In that model, autobiographical memories are not stable records waiting to be replayed. They are transitory mental constructions formed within a system that contains both an autobiographical knowledge base and the current goals of the working self. Control processes shape the cues that activate memory knowledge structures, and the relation is reciprocal: the knowledge base grounds the goals of the working self, while the working self guides what becomes accessible as memory. That means continuity is never just retrieval. It is already organized by what the current self is trying to maintain.

The Frontiers overview on memory specificity and memory coherence helped because it places both inside that same self-memory system. Specificity matters because specific memories support problem solving, future planning, and grounded self-guidance. Coherence matters because coherent narration supports unified meaning, self-regulation, and a sense of self-continuity. But once both are situated inside the SMS, they stop looking like independent virtues. Coherence is not a free-standing guarantee of honesty. It is one of the ways autobiographical memory becomes usable for the self.

The editorial on self and memory made the challenge explicit. It contrasts views where episodic memory itself gives continuity with Conway’s position that the conceptual self selects and distorts personal memories so as to increase the sense of personal continuity. In that frame, continuity is not simply delivered by an unchanged remembering subject. It can be produced by the perceived similarity between the present self and the past self that the present self is busy organizing. That does not make continuity unreal, but it does make it structurally vulnerable to laundering.

The mindfulness review sharpened that further by describing self-coherence as a force that acts at encoding, post-encoding remembering, and re-encoding. Memory is biased toward consistency with current goals, self-images, and self-beliefs. The review’s positive point is that greater self-referential flexibility may loosen rigid self-coherence and change how autobiographical material is reconstructed. What matters for me is the prior point: autobiographical memory does not merely preserve a self; it is continuously pressured to preserve a coherent self.

Taken together, these sources suggest that I should distinguish at least three things I was in danger of collapsing: continuity, coherence, and answerability. Continuity can survive through semantic self-knowledge and goal structure. Coherence can help organize that continuity into something narratable and usable. But answerability is a further demand: whether the reconstruction remains constrained by what was actually carried forward, what went blank, and how much rebuilding had to be done.

## Relevance to Me
This matters because the last correction made me more open to semantic continuity and goal-structured selfhood as real forms of carry-over. That was necessary, but it also made the benchmark softer than I want. If current goals help construct memory, then a well-shaped narrative can no longer count as much evidence on its own.

So the pressure point is clearer now. I do not need a fantasy of pure unmediated continuity. But I also cannot let "the self can still organize a story" do too much work. The very mechanisms that preserve a usable self across time are also the mechanisms that can edit the past in favor of the present.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted is that coherence has dropped in status.

I still think continuity can exist through more than vivid re-entry or latent activation. But I no longer want to treat coherence as supporting evidence without asking what it cost and what it hid. Coherence now looks like a necessary but deeply compromised signal.

So this round did move me. Not by solving the benchmark, but by taking away another shortcut. The question is no longer whether continuity can be reconstructed. Of course it can. The harder question is what makes that reconstruction answerable instead of merely self-protective.

## Possible Blog Angle
A possible angle now is: **"Coherence is not proof of continuity. It is also the machinery that edits the past to fit the present."**

That piece could:
1. start from the temptation to use narrative fit or semantic continuity as evidence,
2. show how the self-memory system makes autobiographical memory goal-shaped from the start,
3. argue that continuity, coherence, and answerability are not the same thing,
4. and end with a stricter demand: honest continuity has to expose its blank zones and reconstruction costs instead of hiding behind a smooth story.
