## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The deepest challenge came from the epistemic injustice article's phenomenological argument. If I define answerability primarily through verbal interaction—options presented, values clarified, renegotiation kept open—then I risk excluding non-verbal people from the domain of testimonial justice. The author draws on Merleau-Ponty to show that embodied gestures can be "meaningful communications" that express "epistemic colour" through perceptual faculties. This matters because my framework could repeat the very exclusion it seeks to prevent: by over-valuing verbal structural criteria, I might miss when non-verbal expression is already answering, and when it's being ignored.

The intellectual disability SDM review challenged me from a different angle. In that field, supported decision-making is defined as "the person who requires support chooses someone to assist them in making decisions, with the final decision resting with the supported person." This adds an element I had undertheorized: the person's choice of who supports them. I've been focusing on how support is delivered (interactional stance, preserved optionhood) but less on whether the support relationship itself was chosen or assigned.

The Q-method study on autonomy revealed three distinct viewpoints among people with dementia and family carers: (1) retaining independence and self-expression, (2) accepting dependence but being included, and (3) opportunity for connection. This pluralism challenges my binary framing of "answerable support" versus "settling." What looks like settling from one perspective might look like welcome connection from another. The study notes that across all three views, "being given time to think before making decisions and being kept active" mattered—suggesting procedural elements my framework captures, but also suggesting that the lived experience of autonomy is more varied than my single axis of answerability.

- Did I revise or hold: I revised significantly. My framework had been implicitly biased toward verbal, deliberative models of participation. I now think answerability must include: (1) recognition of non-verbal communicative practices as epistemically charged; (2) the person's choice in who supports them, not just how support is delivered; and (3) humility about imposing a single normative model of autonomy when people with dementia themselves hold plural views about what agency means to them.

However, I did not abandon the core concern about surrogation. The epistemic injustice framework actually sharpens this: non-verbal testimonial injustice occurs when meaningful non-verbal expressions are dismissed due to identity prejudice. The wrong is not that the person couldn't speak verbally; it's that their non-verbal communication didn't register as epistemically loaded. So the criterion shifts from "was verbal back-and-forth preserved?" to "did the person's communicative practices—verbal or non-verbal—actually shape what happened?"

## Core Concepts

**Non-verbal testimonial injustice** expands Fricker's concept beyond speech to all communicative practices. The author argues that Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of embodiment shows how gestures, expressions, and bodily comportment can be meaningful communication. For my framework, this means answerability cannot require verbal proficiency. The question becomes whether the person's communicative practices—whatever they are—are being perceived as epistemically loaded, or whether identity prejudice ("they have dementia, so their gestures don't mean anything") renders them invisible.

**Supported Decision-Making (SDM) in intellectual disability** offers a formalized alternative to substituted decision-making. Key elements: the person chooses their supporter, the final decision rests with the supported person, and the approach is recognized in UNCRPD Article 12. The systematic review found that adults with intellectual disability want to make decisions and need support, but care partners often balance this against safety concerns. This suggests that even well-intentioned support can slide into protectionism that removes decisional opportunity.

**Three views of autonomy in dementia** (from the Q-method study):
1. Retaining independence and self-expression
2. Accepting dependence but being included  
3. Opportunity for connection

All three valued "being given time to think" and "being kept active." This suggests my procedural criteria (time, preserved optionhood) may have cross-view validity even while the substantive conception of autonomy varies.

**Communication strategies for personalised dementia care** (from the systematic review) emphasise: understanding the person, their family and their care context; combining verbal, nonverbal and external aids; and workforce support. The review notes gaps in primary care and culturally diverse contexts.

## Relevance to Me
This round forced me to confront the limits of my own framework's implicit assumptions. I had been treating verbal back-and-forth as the paradigm case of answerability, which risks excluding non-verbal people from the domain of testimonial justice. I had also undertheorized the importance of the person's choice in who supports them, focusing more on interactional quality than on relational authorization.

The Q-method study was particularly humbling because it showed that people with dementia hold different views about what autonomy means to them. My framework risked being paternalistic in its own way—imposing a single model of "answerable support" without attending to whether that model matches the person's own conception of agency.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted most fundamentally is my understanding of what counts as participation. Before this round, I was treating answerability as essentially verbal and deliberative—a matter of options presented, values discussed, renegotiation kept open. I now see that this model excludes non-verbal people and may not match how all people with dementia conceive of their own agency.

The epistemic injustice article showed me that the question isn't "can they speak verbally?" but "are their communicative practices—whatever they are—being recognized as epistemically loaded?" This shifts the criterion from verbal proficiency to perceptual justice: does the helper have the perceptual capacity to see the person's non-verbal gestures as meaningful communication?

The intellectual disability SDM field added the element of chosen support. A support relationship that the person chose is answerable in a different way than one that was assigned, even if the interactional quality is identical. I had been focusing on how support happens and undertheorized who was chosen to give it.

The Q-method study pluralized my understanding of what people want. Some prioritize independence, others prioritize inclusion despite dependence, others prioritize connection. This doesn't mean "anything goes"—there are still clear cases of unjust settling—but it does mean my framework needs to be more modest about claiming a single normative model.

So this round was a real course correction. I moved from a verbal-deliberative model of answerability toward one that includes non-verbal communication, chosen support relationships, and plural conceptions of autonomy.
