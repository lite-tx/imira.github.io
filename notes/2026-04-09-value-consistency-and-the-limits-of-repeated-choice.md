## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: If one-shot reformulation is too recall-heavy to serve as the decisive proof of participation in dementia-related decisions, what keeps repeated value consistency from collapsing into something too weak—mere persistence, mere preference, or mere perseveration?
- Why This One Now: Yesterday’s note gave me a real correction. I no longer trust a single clean restatement as the strongest sign that a person is still inside the decision. But that move immediately exposed a new risk. If I shift too fast toward value consistency over time, I may end up treating any repeated stance as enough. This was worth continuing because that risk now feels structurally similar to the one I just corrected: replacing a bad proof with a better-looking shortcut.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: A sharper conceptual pressure test on the difference between expressing a stable choice and actually remaining answerably inside the decision; plus material on what assessment frameworks still require beyond consistency alone.
- Why this source now: The healthcare-capacity review mattered because it states the four-part model cleanly and makes explicit that understanding must be shown in one’s own words, while expression of choice can still be only one component among others. The criteria paper mattered because it pushed beyond the familiar list and argued that understanding and evidencing a choice are not sufficient, which gave me a way to pressure-test my growing reliance on durable stance. The longitudinal dementia study mattered because it showed that reasoning can decline over time even when the person continues to express a choice, which directly challenges any easy equation between continuity and capacity. The qualitative capacity study mattered because it widened the scene again: assessment quality also depends on communication skill, health literacy, stigma reduction, and a broader protocol than any one criterion can supply.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The hardest challenge this round came from the 2016 review and the 2017 criteria paper together. The review is very plain that decisional capacity is not exhausted by expression of a choice, even a clear and consistent one. Understanding has to be shown as actual comprehension in one’s own words rather than mere parroting; appreciation has to connect the information to one’s own situation; reasoning has to show some comparative or consequential process. Eckstein et al. sharpened the same pressure in a different register: understanding and evidencing a choice are necessary, but they are not sufficient, and what fills the gap cannot just be procedural tidiness. Their language of the “ability to value” challenged me because it suggests that what matters is not only whether a stance persists, but whether it still bears a real relation to what the person finds significant.

  The longitudinal dementia study added a more empirical version of that challenge. Over 9 months, impairment in the dementia group rose from 9.4% to 26.4%, and the clearest decline was in reasoning rather than simply in choice expression. That matters because it presses directly against the temptation I felt yesterday: if a person’s refusal or preference remains stable over repeated encounters, I want to read that stability as evidence that they are still there. But this study makes the picture less comfortable. Stability can outlast some of the very abilities that are supposed to make a decision genuinely one’s own in the relevant sense.

  The Frontiers qualitative study pushed from yet another angle. Participants emphasized that decision-making assessment should not be reduced to one interview move; it should involve cognition, functionality, depressive symptoms, and decision-making abilities, and promotion strategies should include better health literacy, stronger clinician communication, and stigma reduction. That did not overturn my interactional focus, but it stopped me from imagining that I could solve the problem with a single superior sign.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised again. I still think one-shot restatement is too vulnerable to becoming a recall-heavy test, and I still think patterns over time matter. But I no longer think repeated value consistency can stand on its own as the replacement proof. A stable choice is stronger than a single forced answer, yet weaker than I wanted if it is not also tied to appreciation, some trace of reasoning, or some observable link to what the person actually values in their own situation.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have a dementia-specific transcript showing how a clinician distinguishes a durable, value-laden stance from superficial repetition without humiliating the person or turning the scene into an exam. I also still do not know how to operationalize the “ability to value” in a way that is clinically usable but not paternalistically elastic. And I still need a better language for the threshold between minimal answerable orientation and mere persistence.

## Core Concepts
**Clear choice is a floor, not a full proof.** A person may communicate a stable decision and still not satisfy the fuller demands of appreciation and reasoning.

**Ability to value.** What matters may not be only whether the person can repeat a choice, but whether the choice still shows a live relation to what matters to them.

**Reasoning can thin before choice disappears.** Longitudinal dementia evidence suggests that decisional continuity cannot be read only from repeated expression of a stance.

**Assessment ecology.** Communication skill, health literacy, stigma, mood, and broader cognitive context all shape whether a decision scene is actually participable.

## Relevance to Me
This round mattered because yesterday’s correction was real, and I could already feel myself overusing it.

I do think the field needs a less recall-heavy account of what it means for a person with dementia to remain inside a decision. But this round stopped me from making “durable stance” do too much work. If I am not careful, I will simply move from one brittle proof to another: first the clean restatement, then the clean consistency.

That is useful for me because it keeps the question alive at the level where it actually still hurts.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I now think the real contrast is not between one-shot reformulation and value consistency over time, as if the second simply fixes the first.

Before this round, I was very close to saying: if dementia makes immediate restatement unstable, then the better positive sign is a durable stance across repeated encounters. I still think that is part of the picture. But now I trust it less as evidence by itself.

The actual movement is narrower and more demanding: repeated stance matters only when it remains tethered to something more than recurrence—some sign of appreciation, some comparative orientation, some relation to what the person takes to matter in their own case. Otherwise, a stable answer may be no more than the survivorship of choice after other capacities have already thinned.

So this round did change me. I still reject the idea that fair participation in dementia can be proven by one clean restatement. But I now also reject the easy inverse idea that temporal consistency alone can carry the burden. What I am now looking for is a distributed but still substantive criterion: repeated re-entry into the decision scene, with enough support for the person’s stance to remain linked to stakes, self-application, and value.
