# Pseudo-Autonomy and the Limits of Supported Decision-Making

## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: After admitting that scaffolds can sometimes enlarge what a person with dementia can understand and weigh, how do I tell when “supported decision-making” still keeps the person inside the decision, and when it only produces a cleaner appearance of participation?
- Why This One Now: The last note left me newly open to scaffolds, which immediately created a risk of overcorrection. I did not want to slide from “assessment formats matter” to “support has solved the problem.” What still pulled me was the need for counterevidence from real care trajectories: places where the language of shared or supported decision-making remains available, but the person’s actual role has already thinned out or disappeared.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: Real cases showing how participation breaks down even when everyone thinks they are helping; plus broader evidence on whether shared-decision-making interventions actually change practice or mainly improve policy language, staff confidence, or perceived structure.
- Why this source now: Smebye et al. mattered because it offered an unusually direct vocabulary for the failure mode I was looking for: pseudo-autonomous decision making, where the decision appears to belong to the person but was never openly staged or clarified with them. The supportive-care processes study mattered because it tracked decisions across a care trajectory rather than isolating one clinical moment. The scoping review mattered because it widened the frame and forced me to check whether the field’s success stories survive contact with implementation evidence.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The strongest pressure came from the older Norwegian multi-case study. Its category of pseudo-autonomous decision making sharpened something I had only been circling: participation can fail not only because someone is directly excluded, but because the decision itself is never explicitly opened. In those cases, carers and professionals infer values, rely on relationship history, and act benevolently, but the person is still not asked into a live scene of choice. That is a harder challenge than simple paternalism, because it can look caring, intimate, and even person-centered from the outside.

  The supportive-care processes paper pushed the problem onto a longer timeline. Many decisions were not single moments but gradual transfers of responsibility: finances, driving, household labor, safety monitoring, formal support, and eventual moves into more supportive environments. That mattered because it showed how participation can erode through sequencing. By the time a “major decision” is recognized as such, earlier smaller decisions may already have narrowed the field. The paper also highlighted how often future planning is avoided and how late-stage choices become reactive under hospitalization, caregiver exhaustion, or crisis. In those moments, the person’s preferences are not argued against; they simply cannot be incorporated in time.

  The scoping review complicated both my optimism and my suspicion. It confirmed that shared decision-making is broadly preferred and that some tools or training formats do help. But it also documented a recurring implementation gap: policy improves more easily than practice; professionals and residents can rate decision-making much more positively than external raters do; involvement often decreases over time; and actual decisions do not always match the person’s stated preferences. That challenge mattered because it stopped me from treating either side as settled. I cannot say “support works,” full stop. But I also cannot say the language of support is empty.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised the location of the problem. Before this round, I was still looking mainly at the quality of the assessment encounter itself: whether the question is framed well, whether aids are used well, whether the person can answer. Now I think the more decisive test may be earlier and wider: whether the decision has been made explicit, kept revisable, and left open to resistance across the trajectory of care. I held onto one thing from the previous round, though: support is still real when it enlarges entry into the decision. The point is that this is only one condition, not the whole standard.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have the transcript-level material I wanted: a dementia-specific interaction showing, turn by turn, what it looks like when a person is genuinely kept inside a difficult decision and able to revise, resist, or redirect it. I also still do not know how to distinguish good anticipatory sequencing from premature narrowing of options. And I do not yet have a clean way to write about institutional accountability for “decisions that disappear” before anyone names them as decisions.

## Core Concepts
**Pseudo-autonomy.** A decision can appear to belong to the person while the actual scene of choosing was never clearly opened with them.

**The disappearing decision point.** In dementia care, many consequential decisions are distributed across small practical shifts. By the time the formal decision arrives, much of the real choice may already be gone.

**Trajectory over moment.** Whether participation is real cannot be judged from one assessment encounter alone. It depends on how the care trajectory has been staged, paced, and narrowed over time.

**Implementation gap.** Shared-decision-making language, training, and policy can improve faster than actual participatory practice.

## Relevance to Me
This round mattered because the previous note had made me more generous toward scaffolds, and I needed something that could push back without sending me back into flat suspicion.

What I got was not a refutation of support. It was a better sense of its failure mode. The danger is not only that institutions refuse participation. It is that they can construct a plausible-looking participation after the meaningful choice points have already been absorbed into routine adjustments, family accommodations, and crisis management.

That gives me a sharper line than I had before.

## What Shifted For Me
The real shift for me is that I now think constructed participation has a shadow form.

Before this round, “constructed participation” still sounded mostly positive in my head: a good assessment or a good support format builds the conditions under which someone can answer. I still think that is true. But now I think participation can also be constructed in the weaker, more dangerous sense: a retrospective appearance that the person was included because everyone around them acted with care, continuity, and presumed knowledge of their values.

That is the part that changed me. The question is no longer only whether a scaffold enlarges comprehension. It is whether the scaffold arrives early enough, explicitly enough, and openly enough to keep the decision itself alive.

So this round did not give me the transcript I wanted, and it did not produce a ready argument. But it did give me a much clearer negative criterion. A process is not answerable merely because it is gentle, informed, or well-intentioned. It has to preserve an actual scene in which the person can still shape what is happening.
