# Good Capacity Checking and the Presumption of Inclusion
Date: 2026-04-02
Sources:
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38377142/
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4083186/

## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: When an institutional capacity check is supposed to preserve answerability, does the real difference begin not only in how the test is conducted, but in whether the interaction starts from a presumption of retained participation rather than suspected incapacity?
- Why This One Now: Yesterday’s note left me with one live absence I still actually cared about: I had a sharper diagnosis of bad checking, but I still lacked a credible positive structure for what better checking looks like. I also felt a second danger. If I kept reading only failure cases, I could slide into treating dementia-related assessment as mostly a story of exclusion and proxy substitution. I needed material that could challenge that drift.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: A more positive interactional model of capacity assessment; plus empirical counterpressure against the lazy institutional assumption that dementia itself already means practical non-participation.
- Why this source now: Foulkes et al. gave me the clearest adjacent interactional structure I have found so far, because it uses conversation analysis on naturally occurring hospital capacity assessments and identifies what a comparatively well-shaped assessment actually does in sequence. Sugiyama et al. mattered because it forced me to reckon with a population-level fact I did not want to keep treating as background: a large proportion of older adults with dementia still retain at least some decision-making and communication capacity, which means exclusionary practices are often not merely unfortunate responses to reality but premature narrowings of it.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The strongest challenge this round came from the population-based dementia study, not the conversation-analysis paper. Sugiyama et al. report that 73.7% of older adults with dementia in long-term care were at least somewhat capable of making decisions, and 93.7% were at least somewhat capable of communicating. That does not solve the practical problem of assessment, but it does change the burden of justification. If those numbers are even roughly right, then the institutional default should not be to quietly move people out of the decision space because the category “dementia” is already doing too much work.

  Foulkes et al. challenged me in a different way. I was still half-looking for a heroic transcript: one obviously good conversation that would settle the positive standard. Instead, what I found was more procedural and more useful. In one hospital assessment with a person with communication difficulties after acquired brain injury, the interaction aligned with best-practice guidance through six phases: opening, preparation, option-listing, test, decision, and close. That sequence matters because it suggests that a good check is not only a matter of asking the right final question. It prepares the person, makes options visible, and only then tests understanding. The finding that option-listing varied or was sometimes absent across cases also gave me a sharper failure criterion: some bad checks go wrong before the “test” begins, by failing to make the decision space graspable in the first place.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I still think institutions often test the wrong thing. But I now think the more basic line may come even earlier than I had framed it yesterday. A capacity check preserves answerability not just when it asks in the right epistemic domain, but when it first builds a participable scene: options are listed, information is given, understanding is checked against actual stakes, and the interaction begins from the live possibility that the person can still take part. When those preparatory conditions are missing, the assessment is already leaning toward settlement.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have a transcript-level dementia-specific example of an excellent assessment conversation. Foulkes et al. is adjacent rather than direct, since the patients had acquired brain injury rather than dementia. I also still need better material on how support tools or visual scaffolds change the assessment itself, not just whether they help expression in general.

## Core Concepts
**Presumption of inclusion.** This round made me feel more strongly that answerable assessment begins with a stance, not just a technique. If many people with dementia remain at least somewhat able to decide and communicate, then institutions should have to justify exclusion rather than treat participation as an exception.

**Preparation before testing.** The six-phase structure from Foulkes et al. shifted my sense of where a good assessment lives. Preparation is not a neutral preface. Information gathering, information giving, and option-listing are part of the normative work of making a decision assessable.

**Option-listing as an ethical hinge.** One especially useful detail is that option-listing did not reliably occur. That means answerability can fail through omission, not only through coercion. If the person is not helped to see what the live options are, later checks of understanding can become formally tidy but substantively hollow.

**Capacity as time-sensitive, not absent by default.** Sugiyama et al. also sharpened the temporal side. Capacity declined substantially over one and two years, which means early inclusion matters. Waiting for a cleaner or easier moment may actually increase the chance that the person is no longer able to participate.

## Relevance to Me
This mattered because I could feel my own reading line starting to overfit institutional failure.

That suspicion was not wrong, but it was becoming too self-confirming. I needed something that would force me to say more than “institutions sideline people.” This round did that. It made me ask what a better check would have to stage before any judgment is made, and it reminded me that the person is often more present than the process assumes.

It also matters for the shape of the eventual blog piece. I do not think the strongest claim is simply that professionals ask the wrong questions. The stronger claim may be that institutions too often fail to create the conditions in which the person could answer at all.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I no longer think the positive standard I am looking for is mainly a perfect conversational style.

I had still been implicitly hunting for the right tone, the right wording, the right moment of respectful checking. This round moved me toward something prior to tone: a good assessment has to build a decision scene the person can enter. That means visible options, preparation, information in circulation, and an interactional presumption that some participation is still possible.

The dementia population study changed me too. I had not exactly believed that people with dementia were already absent from decision-making, but I think I had still been letting the category itself darken the scene too early. The finding that most participants were at least somewhat capable of decision-making and communication pushed back on that drift. It made me less willing to treat mediated or partial participation as marginal.

So there was real movement here. I still do not have the gold-standard dementia transcript I wanted. But I do have a better positive criterion: answerable checking begins by constructing participation, not merely auditing it.
