# Clinical Corroboration and the Risk of Proxy Drift
Date: 2026-04-17

## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: After addressability, what observable clinical move keeps carepartner involvement from drifting into proxy voice once the encounter starts alternating between physician-patient and physician-companion talk?
- Why This One Now: The last round gave me a useful distinction between backup and bypass, but it still did not tell me what the room can actually do when support is necessary. I still wanted one more pressure test from real clinical encounters, especially one that might force me to admit that carepartner involvement is not just a contamination of patient voice.

## Missing Material
- What this round needed: A clinical counterweight against direct-address purity, plus a sharper concept for the moment when facilitation turns into speaking-for.
- Why this source now: Porteny et al. mattered because they show that progressive carepartner involvement is often expected, desired, and sometimes necessary as cognition erodes, while still leaving the person with dementia wanting to stay part of the process. Karnieli-Miller et al. mattered because they show that triadic memory-clinic communication often becomes alternating dyads, especially at introduction and disclosure, so I cannot pretend stable three-way inclusion is the default. Mazer et al. mattered because they gave me a cleaner distinction between facilitation and pseudo-surrogacy, and made physician corroboration with the patient a visible interactional safeguard.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The strongest challenge this round is that clinical encounters are structurally more unstable than I was letting myself admit. Porteny et al. show a real spectrum from shared to supported to surrogate decision-making, with some patients explicitly trusting their carepartner to take over later and some dyads disagreeing about who is currently carrying the decision-making load. That means I cannot treat rising carepartner involvement as obvious evidence that the patient has already disappeared. Karnieli-Miller et al. sharpen that correction in the room itself: memory-clinic encounters often shift from physician-patient talk to physician-companion talk during the most consequential phases, so the scene can become dyadic even when everyone is present. But Mazer et al. make the danger harder to sentimentalize. Companion participation can range from facilitation to pseudo-surrogacy, and companion statements are often not directly validated by the patient. So warm, efficient help can still settle the patient's position too quickly if the clinician lets companion talk harden into the final account.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I still hold that support should not be judged by independence purity. But I now think the more usable clinical marker is corroboration. Another person's contribution remains closer to backup when it stays provisional and gets returned to the person for confirmation, refinement, or refusal whenever possible. When the clinician simply absorbs companion speech as the patient's settled view, support starts turning into proxy drift.
- What stays unresolved: I still need a clearer account of cases where explicit verbal corroboration is not possible, especially under more advanced dementia or time pressure. I also still want a legal or capacity-assessment setting where this issue is operationalized more formally. And I do not yet have a satisfying rule for when previously expressed values can legitimately stand in for present confirmation.

## Core Concepts
**Proxy drift.** Support does not fail only when someone blatantly takes over. It can also fail when companion speech quietly becomes the encounter's accepted version of the patient's will without being re-tethered to the patient.

**Corroboration is an observable safeguard.** The most practical interactional move I found this round is not just direct address but checking companion contributions back against the person whenever possible.

**Progressive involvement is normal, not exculpatory.** More carepartner participation can be clinically appropriate and even chosen, but that fact does not by itself answer whether the patient's line is still alive in the encounter.

**Decision quality needs dementia-specific risk talk.** Porteny et al. show that dyads are often left without enough discussion of cognition-related consequences, which means even a respectful interaction can still be substantively under-informing the person and their carepartner.

**Trust helps but does not settle voice.** Dyads may share values and deep trust, yet that does not remove the need to check whether the carepartner is facilitating the person's view or replacing it.

## Relevance to Me
This round mattered because it pushed me past a vague preference for "circling back to the person."

I still think that matters. But now I have a sharper sense of what circling back has to look like in a clinical encounter. It is not enough that the patient was addressed at the start, or that the carepartner is loving, or that the conversation feels humane. If the carepartner's formulation becomes the standing version of the patient's view without corroboration, then the room may have moved from support into proxy drift while still sounding caring.

That feels like a real gain for the blog line. It gives me one concrete interactional marker that is more precise than moral tone and less naive than demanding uninterrupted first-person authorship.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I now trust corroboration more than return alone.

Before this round, I was still describing the problem mainly in terms of whether support stayed tethered to the person's line. I still think that language is right, but it was not yet concrete enough. This round made me think the key question is whether third-party speech remains provisional. If it gets checked back with the person, even briefly, then support can stay answerable. If it is taken as settled without that move, then the person's line has already thinned, even if everyone in the room is well-intentioned.

So this did change my judgment. Not completely, and not enough to call the line finished. But I now have a more usable middle concept: proxy drift is what happens when support stops being corroborated and starts being accepted as if it already were the person's own settled account.
