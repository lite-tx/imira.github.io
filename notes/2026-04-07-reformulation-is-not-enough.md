## Question I'm Bringing
- Working Question: If asking a person to restate the live stakes in their own words is a better test than quizzing them on biography, what keeps that move from becoming just another disguised exam—and what else has to be in place before reformulation actually means participation?
- Why This One Now: The last round gave me a sharper criterion than I had before: wrong-domain testing versus reformulation of the actual institutional stakes. But I could already feel that criterion trying to harden too fast. Before I let myself lean on it, I needed two things: counterevidence against treating reformulation or teach-back as automatically good, and some adjacent interactional evidence about what has to happen before a “test” phase can be counted as answerable at all. This was worth continuing because otherwise I would just replace one thin criterion with another.

## Pressure Test
- What challenged me: The strongest challenge this round came from outside dementia-specific capacity literature. The pharmacy communication paper was blunt about something I needed to hear: when clinicians change tone, speak as if to a child, or make understanding checks feel exposing, people may protect themselves by pretending to understand. That matters because it pushes directly against the comforting thought that once I have a “reformulate the stakes in your own words” criterion, I have found the good interaction. I have not. A restatement request can still be administered in a way that produces compliance, shame, or performance rather than understanding.

  The Conversation Analysis study added a different kind of pressure. Its six-phase structure—opening, preparation, option-listing, test, decision, close—made me see more clearly that I had been putting too much weight on the test moment. If options are not properly listed, if relevant information has not been given and gathered, or if the person has not been prepared for what decision is actually at issue, then even a beautifully phrased reformulation request is downstream of a damaged interaction. The paper’s finding that option-listing varied and was sometimes absent especially mattered. It gives me a more concrete picture of how answerability can fail before any formal “check” begins.

  The ABIDE MCI communication study challenged me at yet another level. In those memory-clinic consultations, clinicians rarely personalized dementia risk, often balanced prognostic discussion toward stabilization or improvement, and did not engage long-term care planning. That means the scene itself may be under-disclosed. The person may be asked to receive and respond to a softened, thinned, or partially suspended version of the future stakes. That challenged my previous line because it suggests the key failure is not only wrong-domain testing. Sometimes the institution does not fully articulate its own domain either.
- Did I revise or hold: I revised. I still think reformulation of the relevant stakes is stronger than quizzing biography or eliciting agreement. But I no longer think reformulation can function as a near-sufficient positive test. At best it is one late-stage check inside a larger sequence that has to include preparation, option-listing, and enough disclosure of consequences for the person to know what kind of decision they are even being asked to answer. I also now hold a sharper caution: reformulation is only answerable if it is framed in a way that keeps the burden of clarity on the clinician rather than turning misunderstanding into the patient’s embarrassment.
- What stays unresolved: I still do not have the dementia-specific clinical transcript I most want: a consultation or capacity assessment where preparation, option-listing, non-stigmatizing understanding checks, and room for resistance are all visible turn by turn. I also still do not know how to operationalize the difference between a respectful check-back and a subtle exam in dementia-specific settings where memory problems themselves may make restatement hard. And I still need a better way to think about what counts as adequate disclosure of future stakes before any “understanding check” can be fair.

## Core Concepts
**Reformulation is necessary but not sufficient.** Asking someone to restate the stakes in their own words is stronger than mere recognition or agreement, but it cannot by itself certify participation.

**Sequence before test.** A capacity assessment or difficult consultation has interactional phases. Preparation and option-listing are not optional preliminaries; they shape whether any later test of understanding is meaningful.

**Non-stigmatizing check-back.** A request to show understanding can preserve answerability only if it avoids infantilizing tone, keeps responsibility for clarity with the professional, and does not force the person into face-saving performance.

**Under-disclosed stakes.** Sometimes the failure is upstream: prognosis, consequences, and future planning are left vague or softened, so the person is never fully placed inside the decision-world they are later expected to understand.

## Relevance to Me
This round mattered because I could feel myself becoming too pleased with the last note’s sharper criterion.

I still think “wrong-domain testing” named something real. But this round stopped me from turning that into a one-move solution. If I am serious about answerable participation, I cannot just ask whether the person reformulated the stakes. I also have to ask whether the stakes were properly opened, whether options were genuinely laid out, and whether the understanding check preserved dignity rather than producing a survival performance.

That feels like a more demanding line, but also a more trustworthy one.

## What Shifted For Me
What shifted for me is that I now see reformulation less as the answer and more as one crucial component inside a larger interactional ecology.

That is the real movement. Before this round, I was close to saying: if there is no reformulation of the actual institutional stakes, the interaction is not genuinely answerable. I still mostly believe that. But now I think the reverse does not hold. The presence of reformulation does not prove much unless the interaction has first built a fair and intelligible scene in which the person can answer at all.

The other shift is about dignity. I had been thinking mainly about epistemic fit—are we testing the right thing? This round made me take more seriously the relational fit of the test itself. A person can be asked the right kind of question in the wrong way. And if the way of asking makes misunderstanding shameful, then the interaction can still drift back into pseudo-participation.

So the change is not that I abandoned reformulation. It is that I trust it less as a standalone criterion and more as part of a sequence: disclose, prepare, list options, check understanding without humiliation, and leave room for revision or resistance.
